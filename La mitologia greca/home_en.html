Origins

These myths, narrated in works like Hesiod's "Theogony," form the basis of Greek cosmogony, explaining not only the origin of the world and the gods but also the structure of the universe and the power dynamics among the various divine entities.

The Primordial Chaos

At the beginning of time, according to Greek mythology, there existed only Chaos, a primordial void without form. From Chaos emerged various primordial entities: Gaia (the Earth), Tartarus (the deep underworld), Eros (love and life force), Erebus (darkness), and Nyx (night).

The Birth of Gaia and Uranus

Gaia, the Earth, gave birth to Uranus, the Sky, who became her consort. From the union of Gaia and Uranus, the first divine beings were born: the Titans, the Cyclopes, and the Hecatoncheires (giants with a hundred arms and fifty heads).

The Rebellion of Cronus

Uranus, fearing the power of his children, imprisoned them within Gaia's womb, provoking her wrath. Gaia persuaded her youngest son, Cronus, to rebel. Armed with a sickle, Cronus castrated his father Uranus and freed his siblings. From the blood of Uranus spilled upon the Earth, the Furies (goddesses of vengeance) and the Giants were born.

The Birth of Zeus and the Gods' Rebellion

When Rhea bore her sixth child, Zeus, she hid him on the island of Crete and deceived Cronus by giving him a stone wrapped in swaddling clothes. Zeus grew up in secret and, once grown, forced Cronus to vomit up his siblings. Together, the rebellious gods declared war on Cronus and the Titans, a war known as the Titanomachy.

The Titanomachy

The war between the Olympian gods, led by Zeus, and the Titans, led by Cronus, was epic and devastating. After a long battle, Zeus and his allies prevailed. The defeated Titans were imprisoned in Tartarus, a deep abyss in the underworld.

The Age of the Olympian Gods

After the victory, Zeus became the ruler of the sky and the gods, Poseidon obtained dominion over the sea, and Hades took control of the underworld. Thus began the age of the Olympian gods, who dwelled on Mount Olympus and ruled the world with powers and influences over various aspects of life and nature.

Deities

These gods inhabit Mount Olympus, considered the sacred place where they reside and from which they rule the world. Their power extends over various aspects of human life and nature, and the myths surrounding them have been passed down through epic poetry, dramas, and artworks of ancient Greece, deeply influencing Western culture.

Zeus

Zeus is the supreme ruler of Mount Olympus and the god of the sky and thunder. He is known for his justice but also for his numerous loves and children, both divine and mortal. As the head of the gods, Zeus maintains order and justice among gods and men.

  • Role:

    King of the gods, god of the sky and thunder.

  • Symbols:

    Thunderbolt, eagle, oak.

Poseidon

Poseidon is the god who rules over all waters. He is the brother of Zeus and Hades and has a violent and unpredictable temperament, reflected in his dominion over the seas and earthquakes.

  • Role:

    God of the sea, earthquakes, and horses.

  • Symbols:

    Trident, horse, dolphin.

Hades

Hades reigns over the underworld, the realm of the dead. Although often associated with death, Hades is not an evil figure but rather the keeper of the souls of the deceased. He does not dwell on Mount Olympus but in his underworld realm.

  • Role:

    God of the underworld and the dead.

  • Symbols:

    Scepter, Cerberus, cypress.

Hera

Hera is the wife of Zeus and queen of the gods. She is revered as the protector of marriage and married women. Despite her position of power, Hera is often depicted as jealous and vengeful towards Zeus's lovers and illegitimate children.

  • Role:

    Queen of the gods, goddess of marriage and family.

  • Symbols:

    Peacock, pomegranate, diadem.

Athena

Athena, born from the head of Zeus, is one of the most revered goddesses. Patroness of Athens, she is the goddess of wisdom, justice, and strategic warfare, differing from Ares for her intelligent and strategic approach to conflicts.

  • Role:

    Goddess of wisdom, strategic warfare, and the arts.

  • Symbols:

    Owl, olive, helmet, and shield.

Aphrodite

Aphrodite is the goddess of love and beauty, born from the sea foam. She is known for her irresistible beauty and the numerous love myths involving her.

  • Role:

    Goddess of love, beauty, and sexuality.

  • Symbols:

    Dove, myrtle, seashell.

Ares

Ares represents the violence and brutality of war. Son of Zeus and Hera, he is often in conflict with Athena, who represents a more strategic and rational aspect of warfare.

  • Role:

    God of war.

  • Symbols:

    Spear, helmet, vulture.

Artemis

Artemis, twin sister of Apollo, is the goddess of the hunt and wild nature. She is also associated with the moon and the protection of young women.

  • Role:

    Goddess of the hunt, wild nature, and the moon.

  • Symbols:

    Bow and arrows, deer, moon.

Apollo

Apollo, son of Zeus and Leto and twin brother of Artemis, is the god of the sun, music, poetry, and healing. He is also the god of prophecies and patron of the Oracle of Delphi.

  • Role:

    God of the sun, music, poetry, and prophecy.

  • Symbols:

    Lyre, laurel, sun.

Demeter

Demeter is the goddess of agriculture, fertility, and harvest. She is the mother of Persephone, whose story of abduction by Hades and subsequent liberation explains the cycle of seasons.

  • Role:

    Goddess of agriculture and fertility.

  • Symbols:

    Sheaves of wheat, torch, pig.

Dionysus

Dionysus is the god of wine, festivities, and ecstasy. He is often depicted as a joyful and liberating god, bringing happiness and chaos with the power of wine and celebration.

  • Role:

    God of wine, festivities, and ecstasy.

  • Symbols:

    Vine, wine cup, panther.

Myths

These myths not only shaped the religion and culture of ancient Greece but also influenced Western literature, art, and philosophy. Through epic narratives, theatrical dramas, and artworks, Greek myths explored universal themes such as love, revenge, courage, and fate, offering moral lessons and explanations for natural phenomena and human experiences.

Prometheus and the Fire

Prometheus, a Titan, stole fire from the gods to give it to humans, teaching them arts and sciences. For this act of rebellion, Zeus punished him by chaining him to a rock, where an eagle would eat his liver every day, which would regenerate every night. Prometheus symbolizes courage and rebellion against divine oppression.

Pandora and the Box

To punish humanity after Prometheus stole fire, Zeus created Pandora, the first woman, and gave her a jar (often interpreted as a box) containing all the evils of the world. When Pandora opened the jar, all the evils were released, but she managed to keep Hope inside, thus offering comfort to humanity.

Hercules and the Twelve Labors

Hercules, renowned for his superhuman strength, was sentenced to perform twelve impossible labors as penance for killing his family in a fit of madness induced by Hera. These labors included slaying the Nemean Lion, capturing the Erymanthian Boar, cleaning the Augean Stables in a single day, and obtaining the Apples of the Hesperides. By completing these feats, Hercules became a symbol of courage and endurance.

Orpheus and Eurydice

Orpheus, the legendary musician, was in love with Eurydice. When Eurydice died, Orpheus descended into the Underworld to bring her back. He charmed Hades and Persephone with his music, gaining permission to bring Eurydice back to life, on the condition that he wouldn't look back at her until they returned to the world of the living. However, Orpheus looked back too soon, losing Eurydice forever.

Theseus and the Minotaur

Theseus, prince of Athens, volunteered to kill the Minotaur, a monster half-man and half-bull that lived in the Labyrinth of Crete. With the help of Ariadne, who gave him a thread to find his way out, Theseus managed to kill the Minotaur and free Athens from the tribute of youths it had to pay to Crete.

Places

These mythological places not only serve as the settings of many Greek myths but also represent symbols and metaphors for various aspects of human life and the natural world. They reflect the beliefs of the ancient Greeks regarding divinity, heroism, justice, and the afterlife, and have had a lasting impact on Western culture and literature.

Mount Olympus

Mount Olympus is the dwelling place of the Olympian gods, located in northern Greece. It is the place where the gods lived, ruled, and celebrated. Known as the highest point in Greece, Olympus was considered a sacred place and inaccessible to mortals. Here resided Zeus and the other major deities, each with their own palace.

Tartarus

Tartarus is a deep abyss located in the underworld, even lower than Hades. It is the place of punishment for defeated titans and other evil entities. In Tartarus, the souls of the damned suffered eternal punishments, overseen by monstrous creatures like the Hundred-Handers and the Giants.

Elysian Fields

The Elysian Fields, or Elysium, represent the afterlife for virtuous souls and heroes. Located at the edges of the known world, these fields are described as a place of eternal spring and happiness, where blessed souls enjoy peace and serenity for eternity. It is the ultimate reward for those who have lived lives of great merit and value.

The Labyrinth of Crete

The Labyrinth of Crete was designed by the skilled craftsman Daedalus for King Minos. This intricate maze housed the Minotaur, a creature half-man and half-bull. Every year, Athens had to send seven young men and seven young women as tribute, who were fed to the Minotaur until the hero Theseus managed to kill it and escape with the help of Ariadne and her thread.

The Garden of the Hesperides

This garden is known for its golden apples, guarded by the nymphs Hesperides and the dragon Ladon. Hercules was tasked with stealing the apples as one of his twelve labors. The garden was located at the western edges of the known world, and was a place of enchanted beauty and abundance.

The Temple of Delphi

Delphi was considered the center of the world in Greek mythology, home to the Oracle of Delphi, the priestess Pythia who gave prophecies inspired by the god Apollo. The temple of Apollo at Delphi was a sacred place of great religious and cultural significance, where Greeks would go to receive divine guidance.
